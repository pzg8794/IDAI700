The actual business model:
1. Build tool with minimal scientific rigor
2. Market deceptively (brand as "proven," "scientifically validated," "reducing bias")
3. Sell to desperate companies drowning in applications
4. Extract maximum revenue while hiding validation data behind NDAs

**Key Finding**: Companies knew about failures. Facebook (Meta) knew their algorithm caused teen suicide. They continued because revenue increased. They only changed when a whistleblower exposed them and external pressure mounted.

**Implication**: Corporations have zero incentive to:
- Validate claims rigorously

This is systemic, not individual corruption. Market pressure incentivizes deeper deception.

### Question 2: Why AI Echoes Phrenology

Schellmann documents that modern AI hiring tools make identical philosophical assumptions to discredited 18th-19th century pseudosciences: inner character manifests in outer appearance; scientific instruments can extract this information reliably.

**The Parallel**:
- Phrenology read skull bumps; physiognomy read facial features; Lombroso identified "criminal types" by appearance
- Modern AI reads facial micro-expressions, voice tone, game behavior, social media patterns
- Both share essentialism (stable inner traits manifest externally) + technological determinism (instruments remove human bias)

**What Makes This Vendor Strategy**: They don't advertise "We use discredited 19th-century logic with 21st-century technology." They market "cutting-edge AI," relying on public ignorance of history.

**Your Understanding**: AI doesn't invent dehumanization through camouflage—it provides the technology vendors need to hide what society already rejected.

## PART 3: "THE HUMAN COST" — Beyond Philosophical Frameworks

### Question 1: What Is Dehumanization?

**Your Technical Correction to Fritts/Cabrera**:

They say AI operates "without understanding." This is technically imprecise and weakens their argument.

**Your Version**: AI operates with **bounded understanding**—understanding only what training data and design parameters teach it. The problem isn't that AI lacks understanding; it's that **its understanding is constrained to metrics while human understanding extends to meaning**.

**Why This Matters**:
- Technically accurate (establishes your authority as someone who builds AI systems)
- Philosophically sharper (reveals structural limitation, not fixable flaw)
- More dangerous implication (no training data can make algorithm understand what humans understand through embodied, relational, contextual judgment)
- Connects to your diagnostic work (CLD adolescents aren't failed by algorithms that "don't understand"; they're failed by algorithms understanding only dominant-culture markers while remaining blind to cultural brilliance)

### Question 2: How Do AI Systems Treat People Differently?

**Your Rejection of the False Binary**

Before tonight's dialogue, you might have answered within Fritts/Cabrera's framework. But you recognized: the question itself is poorly framed and distracts from real problems.

**The False Comparison**:
- "AI treats people differently from humans" — technically true but irrelevant
- Assumes neutrality between "AI treatment" and "human treatment" as if they're equivalent choices
- Ignores that both AI AND humans can abuse

**The Real Problem**:
Companies don't hesitate to use human interviewers who belittle candidates (JetBlue example). They actively use hiring processes that systematize abuse through opacity. The question isn't "which is better" but "how do we prevent abuse systematically?"

**Your Insight**: Fritts/Cabrera worry that AI removes human presence. Your experience: human presence can be weaponized. An algorithm would never conduct the JetBlue interview. An algorithm would never belittle you, make you feel stupid, or waste your time asking the same unanswerable question repeatedly.

**The Actual Question You're Asking**: "How can AI be designed to protect people from human cruelty in hiring?" Not: "Does AI treat people worse than humans?"

## PART 4: EVALUATION — What Authors Miss & What Matters

### Strengths of Each Approach

**Schellmann's Empirical Rigor**:
- Documents actual failures with specificity (Amazon's "women's" bias, HireVue contradictions, Pymetrics game irrelevance)
- Evidence-based, not theoretical hand-waving
- Investigative journalism reveals what companies hide

**Fritts & Cabrera's Philosophical Clarity**:
- Names dehumanization as distinct from bias
- Shows why "fair algorithms" don't solve the problem
- Clarifies that human presence isn't automatically good (just different from algorithmic presence)

### Limitations of Both

**False Binary Framing**:
Both blame AI for problems humans already systematized. They create opposition (AI vs. human) that obscures the real issue (systemic dehumanization of any origin).

**Missing Solutions**:
- Neither addresses transparency/education/governance frameworks
- Ask "should we use AI?" instead of "how do we design ethical systems?"
- Insufficient analysis of power and systemic dehumanization already embedded in human processes

**Insufficient Scope**:
- Schellmann: Investigative but not solutions-oriented
- Fritts/Cabrera: Philosophical but disconnected from governance mechanisms that actually change behavior (tax incentives, mandatory oversight, regulatory accountability)

### The Actual Question

Not: "Whether AI or humans dehumanize better?"

**But**: "How do we design transparent, equitable, community-partnered systems that protect people from both algorithmic and human abuse?"

This is where your EQUITAS framework enters:
- Transparency: Show what criteria are being measured
- Education: Teach people to interpret those criteria
- Community Partnership: Let affected people shape the framework
- Passion Detection: Acknowledge some human qualities require human understanding, but design systems that don't hide what they're measuring
