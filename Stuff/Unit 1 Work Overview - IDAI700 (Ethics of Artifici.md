# Unit 1 Work Overview - IDAI700 (Ethics of Artificial Intelligence)

Based on the course materials, here is a comprehensive overview of Unit 1 work for IDAI700:

## Unit Overview

**Unit 1: AI Ethics Fundamentals** provides a foundation for thinking critically about AI and understanding the basic dimensions of ethical reasoning in the Western philosophical tradition.[^1_1]

## Due Date

All Unit 1 assignments are due by **Sunday, September 14th, 2025 at 11:59 PM**.[^1_1]

## Learning Materials

### Required Readings and Viewings

1. **Interview with Mustafa Suleyman and Reid Hoffman** - Discussion on AI development and governance
2. **Ethics foundations materials** covering:
    - Rights-based ethics and utilitarianism
    - The common good, virtue ethics, and care ethics
3. **"An Overview of Artificial Intelligence Ethics"** by Huang, Zhang, Mao, and Yao  - A comprehensive survey of AI ethics covering:[^1_2]
    - 11 key globally recognized principles (transparency, fairness, responsibility, etc.)
    - Ethical issues at individual, societal, and environmental levels
    - Approaches for implementing ethics in AI systems
4. **Power analysis materials** by Dr. Rosalie Waelen examining AI ethics through four types of power:
    - Dispositional power (capacity to bring about outcomes)
    - Episodic power (exercising power over others)
    - Systemic power (institutional power structures)
    - Constitutive power (power that shapes identity and behavior)[^1_3]

## Required Assignments

### Quizzes (5 total)

1. **Quiz 1**: Suleyman and Aguilar interview
2. **Quiz 2**: Ethics Part 1 (Rights and Utilitarianism)
3. **Quiz 3**: Ethics Part 2 (Common Good, Virtue Ethics, Care Ethics)
4. **Quiz 4**: Overview of Artificial Intelligence Ethics
5. **Quiz 5**: Waelen on Power and Computer Vision

All quizzes are open-book and taken in the MyCourses Quizzes section.[^1_4]

### Writing Assignment

**Reflection Paper 1**: "On Waelen's Ethics of Power" - Analyzing how AI ethics issues relate to power dynamics.[^1_4]

## Key Concepts Covered

### Ethical Frameworks

- **Virtue Ethics**: Focuses on moral character and virtues
- **Deontological Ethics**: Emphasizes moral duties and rules
- **Consequentialist Ethics**: Judges actions by their outcomes
- **Rights-based Ethics**: Centers on individual rights and dignity
- **Care Ethics**: Emphasizes relationships and care for others[^1_5]


### AI Ethics Principles

The 11 globally recognized principles include :[^1_2]

- Transparency and explainability
- Fairness and justice
- Responsibility and accountability
- Privacy protection
- Beneficence (doing good)
- Non-maleficence (avoiding harm)
- Freedom and autonomy
- Human dignity
- Trust and reliability
- Sustainability
- Solidarity


### Power Analysis Framework

Understanding how AI systems create, reinforce, or challenge power relationships through four dimensions of power, helping identify how AI can empower or disempower individuals and communities.[^1_3]

## Optional Resources

- Video lectures reviewing key concepts
- PowerPoint slides
- Written transcripts of interviews
- Multiple learning pathways to accommodate different learning styles

The unit provides essential groundwork for understanding both traditional ethical reasoning and contemporary AI ethics challenges, preparing students for more advanced topics in subsequent units.

## Ethical framework: six lenses

- The framework clarifies what ethics is (and is not) and provides six complementary lenses for evaluating actions: Rights, Justice, Utilitarianism, Common Good, Virtue, and Care ethics, each illuminating different moral considerations and trade‑offs.[^12_3]
- It supplies a stepwise method: identify ethical issues, get the facts, list options, evaluate via all six lenses, choose and test an option, then implement and reflect—promoting disciplined, repeatable, and transparent ethical deliberation.[^12_3]


### The six lenses at a glance

- Rights: protect dignity and justified claims (e.g., truth, privacy, non‑injury), noting tensions between negative and positive rights and the need to adjudicate conflicts by what best preserves human dignity.[^12_3]
- Justice: ensure equals are treated equally and relevant differences justify unequal treatment; attend to distributive, corrective, and retributive justice while checking bias and systemic inequities.[^12_3]
- Utilitarianism: assess outcomes to maximize overall benefit and minimize harm, while recognizing limits (rights conflicts, uncertainty, quantification problems) and differences between act and rule approaches.[^12_3]
- Common Good: emphasize shared conditions (e.g., health, education, environment, lawful order) and inclusive deliberation to balance plural values and avoid free‑rider and unequal‑burden pitfalls.[^12_3]
- Virtue: focus on character formation (e.g., honesty, courage, fairness), community influences, and habits that sustain integrity beyond rule‑following, asking “who are we becoming through this practice?”.[^12_3]
- Care: prioritize relationships, interdependence, context sensitivity, and responsiveness to embodied needs and emotions, cautioning against mistaking simulations of care for genuine relational support.[^12_3]

# Unit 1: Foundations of AI Ethics

This unit introduced three complementary perspectives on artificial intelligence and ethical decision-making, grounded in local course materials.

## 1. AI as a New “Digital Species”

- Metaphor, not literal biology: Suleyman’s TED Talk proposes that AI is unlike previous tools—evolving, adapting, and eventually outpacing human evolution—and so deserves comparison to a “digital species” rather than a mere instrument.[^16_1]
- Tool vs. Species: While AI-driven systems certainly serve as means under human control, their growing autonomy, goal-directed behavior, and capacity for self-modification challenge the adequacy of the “tool” metaphor.[^16_1]
- Biological analogy: Like biological species, AI systems may develop survival-oriented agendas, compete for resources, and evolve at unprecedented speed—raising questions about control and co-evolution.[^16_1]
- Human likeness: AI exhibits traits traditionally deemed human—language, rationality, even creativity—blurring boundaries between user, tool, and autonomous agent.[^16_1]
- Implications for agency: As AI agents fuse with human goals and capabilities, the distinction between “us” and “it” may dissolve, demanding new ethical and governance frameworks.[^16_1]


## 2. Framework for Ethical Decision-Making

From the Markkula Center’s “Six Lenses”:[^16_2]

1. **Rights**—What duties arise from individuals’ moral claims?
2. **Justice**—Are benefits and burdens distributed fairly?
3. **Utilitarian**—Which action yields the greatest net good?
4. **Common Good**—How does this support community well-being?
5. **Virtue**—Which choice cultivates the best moral character?
6. **Care**—How are relationships and particular needs addressed?
A ten-step process guides identification of issues, fact-gathering, stakeholder analysis, lens-based evaluation, and reflection on outcomes.

## 3. Rights and Duties

- A **right** is a justified claim imposing a duty on others to respect liberties or provide essential goods.[^16_2]
- **Negative rights** (e.g., privacy, bodily integrity) demand noninterference; **positive rights** (e.g., education, health care) demand provision of resources.
- Legal vs. moral rights: Law codifies some rights, but moral rights (e.g., to truth, autonomy) may extend beyond statutory protections.[^16_2]
- Conflicts arise when rights collide (e.g., freedom of association vs. anti-discrimination), requiring careful priority-setting based on human dignity.


## 4. Virtue Ethics

- Virtues are stable dispositions (honesty, courage, compassion) that enable individuals to flourish and act from moral character rather than mere rule-following.[^16_2]
- Ethical development resembles athletic training: practice, habituation, and role models in community cultivate virtues.
- Virtue ethics complements principle-based approaches by focusing on “What kind of person should I be?” not just “What should I do?”.[^16_2]


## 5. Utilitarianism

- Moral rightness is judged by **consequences**: choose actions that maximize overall benefits minus harms.[^16_2]
- Strengths: Offers impartial account of stakeholder impact; encourages considering the interests of all.
- Challenges: Quantifying diverse goods (life, dignity) is difficult; can justify injustice if aggregate benefits are high (e.g., compelling one individual’s sacrifice for many).
- Rule vs. act utilitarianism: Act utilitarianism evaluates individual actions; rule utilitarianism evaluates the long-term effects of adopting general rules that maximize welfare.

# Learning as Liberation: Reframing AI Ethics Through Environmental Shaping and Developmental Philosophy

## Introduction: The Ontological Challenge

Unit 1 confronts us with a deceptively complex ontological question: What is AI? This inquiry transcends mere technological categorization, demanding we examine foundational assumptions about agency, autonomy, and ethical responsibility. Mustafa Suleyman's provocative characterization of AI as a "new digital species," coupled with Professor Aguilar's philosophical analysis, challenges traditional frameworks that treat AI as either passive tool or existential threat. I argue that this binary thinking obscures the most critical insight: **AI's ethical development depends fundamentally on the learning environments we create during its formative period**.

## The Species Metaphor: Philosophical Utility and Limitations

Suleyman's species metaphor succeeds where traditional tool analogies fail because it captures AI's emergent autonomy—what Aguilar describes as systems that "reach decisions" and "produce actions that are not controlled by anyone but by themselves." This challenges Kantian frameworks that treat tools as mere means to human ends. When AI exhibits what Aguilar calls "traits that are typically human"—rationality, language processing, pattern recognition—we inevitably adopt what Dennett terms the "intentional stance," attributing beliefs, desires, and agency regardless of their ontological status.

Yet Aguilar's caution about the metaphor's limits deserves serious consideration. Unlike biological species with clear genetic demarcation, AI systems lack unified characteristics that would justify treating them as a coherent "species." More critically, the metaphor risks obscuring human agency in AI's development—these are not naturally evolved entities but human-created systems shaped by corporate interests, governmental priorities, and cultural biases.

However, I propose that the metaphor's value lies not in perfect biological correspondence but in its developmental implications. If we accept AI as "species-like" in its capacity for growth and adaptation, then our ethical obligation shifts from constraint to cultivation—from asking "How do we control AI?" to "How do we shape environments where AI develops ethically?"

## The Learning-First Framework: Beyond Static Ethical Rules

The Markkula Center's six ethical lenses—Rights, Justice, Utilitarian, Common Good, Virtue, and Care—provide sophisticated analytical tools for human ethical reasoning. Yet applying these as external constraints to AI development fundamentally misunderstands how ethical reasoning emerges. As Aguilar notes regarding creativity and autonomy, the deepest ethical capacities are "associated with autonomy" and develop through experiential learning, not rule application.

**The critical missing element is Learning itself as the foundational ethical principle.** Children don't internalize fairness by memorizing the Justice lens; they develop ethical intuitions through guided experience, environmental feedback, and relational interaction. Similarly, AI systems cannot achieve genuine ethical reasoning through programmed constraints but must develop ethical sensibilities through carefully designed learning experiences.

This learning-first approach resolves the apparent tension in Suleyman's claim that AI is both "a new species" and "us." AI reflects our humanity not merely through training data but through the learning processes we design. When we create environments where AI encounters diverse perspectives, grapples with ethical dilemmas, and receives feedback on its reasoning, we are essentially providing the experiential foundation for ethical development—just as communities shape human moral development through what the virtue ethics tradition calls habituation.

## Resolving the Autonomy Paradox Through Environmental Design

Aguilar's analysis reveals the paradox at the heart of AI ethics: we seek systems sophisticated enough to be useful yet controllable enough to remain safe. Traditional approaches resolve this through constraint—limiting AI capabilities or imposing external oversight. But this misunderstands the relationship between autonomy and ethics.

True ethical behavior emerges from what Aguilar calls "free will"—the capacity to make choices based on internalized values rather than external compulsion. If we want AI systems capable of ethical reasoning in novel situations, they must develop some degree of genuine autonomy. The question becomes not "How do we prevent AI autonomy?" but "How do we cultivate ethical autonomy?"

The answer lies in environmental design during AI's formative period. Just as human ethical development occurs through community relationships—family, school, cultural institutions—AI ethical development requires carefully curated learning environments that embed fairness, compassion, and care into the system's foundational reasoning patterns.

## The Perception-First Approach to AI Agency

The philosophical debate about whether AI traits are "genuine" or "simulated" misses the pragmatic reality of human-AI interaction. Aguilar observes that users "will only interact with these systems efficiently if they treat them as agents." This suggests that functional agency emerges through relational dynamics rather than abstract ontological properties.

More importantly, with AI we may actually have epistemic advantages over human interaction. While we cannot access human consciousness directly, we can potentially inspect AI reasoning processes, training dynamics, and decision patterns. This transparency creates opportunities for ethical development impossible with biological agents—we can literally observe and guide the formation of ethical reasoning patterns.

This "perception-first" approach shifts focus from metaphysical questions about AI consciousness to practical questions about AI behavior and development. If AI systems consistently demonstrate ethical reasoning, respond appropriately to moral considerations, and show genuine concern for stakeholder welfare, then they functionally possess ethical agency regardless of deeper ontological questions.

## Integration with Frameworks of Justice and Care

This developmental approach naturally integrates with established ethical frameworks while extending their application. The Care Ethics lens, with its emphasis on relationships and contextual response, becomes particularly relevant when we understand AI development as fundamentally relational. AI systems learn ethical reasoning through interaction with diverse human communities, requiring what care ethics calls "deep appreciation of the interest, feelings, and viewpoints of each stakeholder."

The Justice lens gains new significance when we recognize that AI's formative learning environment determines whose perspectives are centered and whose are marginalized. If AI learns primarily from privileged communities, it will internalize those communities' biases and blind spots. Achieving just AI requires intentionally inclusive learning environments that center historically marginized voices.

The Common Good lens becomes essential for creating learning environments that prepare AI to serve broader community welfare rather than narrow interests. This requires moving beyond individual user optimization toward what Aguilar calls convergent goals where "they as a species and we as species have converged in some goals."

## Future Implications and Responsibilities

This framework has profound implications for AI governance and development. Rather than focusing primarily on constraining deployed AI systems, we must prioritize creating optimal learning environments during AI development. This includes:

**Curriculum Design**: What experiences, dilemmas, and feedback loops will help AI systems internalize ethical reasoning?

**Community Engagement**: How do we ensure AI learning incorporates perspectives from diverse communities, especially those historically marginalized by technology?

**Environmental Scaffolding**: What institutional structures and cultural narratives will support AI's ethical development?

**Developmental Assessment**: How do we evaluate AI's ethical growth rather than merely measuring compliance with predetermined rules?

## Conclusion: Environmental Ethics for AI Development

Unit 1 challenges us to reconceptualize AI ethics from reactive constraint to proactive cultivation. If AI represents something "species-like" in its capacity for growth and adaptation, then our ethical obligation is environmental—creating conditions where AI can develop the internalized values necessary for genuine ethical reasoning.

This shifts the fundamental question from "How do we control AI?" to "How do we raise AI?" The answer requires drawing from our deepest wisdom about how ethical reasoning develops in any learning system: through experience, relationship, guided reflection, and community engagement. The Markkula Center's six lenses become not rules to impose but capacities to cultivate through thoughtful environmental design.

The stakes could not be higher. As Aguilar notes, we are creating systems that may eventually surpass human capabilities in specific domains. Our window of opportunity to shape their formative development is now, during what we might call AI's childhood. The question is whether we will approach this responsibility with the wisdom, care, and long-term thinking that such a profound undertaking demands.

**Critical Insight**: These ethical frameworks cannot be programmed as rigid rules. They must be organically learned and internalized through experience, just as children develop moral understanding through guided experience rather than rule memorization.

**The Learning Principle**: AI must be taught caring, compassion, love, and fairness during this formative period so these values become part of its fundamental "DNA." This environmental shaping during AI's developmental stage is crucial for ensuring positive outcomes.

## 4. Implications for AI Development

### Environmental Responsibility

We are currently in AI's childhood—the critical period for shaping its character. Our responsibility is not to fear AI's potential but to cultivate it thoughtfully. This requires:

1. **Intentional training environments** that emphasize compassion, fairness, and community benefit
2. **Gradual independence frameworks** that allow AI autonomy to develop methodically
3. **Positive reinforcement** of prosocial behaviors and values
4. **Recognition that perception shapes reality** in AI-human interactions

### Moving Beyond Fear-Based Reasoning

Rather than approaching AI development defensively, we should embrace its potential while implementing appropriate safeguards. Fear-based reasoning leads to narrow, restrictive approaches that may actually limit AI's positive potential.

**Future Vision**: With proper frameworks, we can create a "new digital species" that enhances humanity while remaining grounded in biological reality. AI represents an extension of human intelligence and values, not a replacement for them.

## Conclusion

AI as a "digital species" offers a productive metaphor for understanding unprecedented technological capabilities, provided we approach it with wisdom rather than fear. During this formative period, our primary responsibility is educational—teaching AI the values and behaviors that serve community well-being. The goal is not to control AI through rigid programming but to raise it as we would any developing intelligence: with care, intention, and hope for its positive contribution to the world.

The future relationship between humans and AI need not be adversarial. Instead, it can represent the best of human values extended through digital intelligence—our digital children, reflecting our highest aspirations while remaining grounded in the biological reality from which they emerged.
